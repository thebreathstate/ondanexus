Cross-Storm Comparison: Ian · Irma · Michael · Florence

A comparative view of recurring execution dynamics across storms with different footprints, markets, and recovery structures.

By Bob Pudlock, Onda Nexus • Published: 2026-02-08

Comparative lens Recurring patterns Execution readiness

This synthesis is derived from the project’s GSPS case studies and is written for public, practitioner-facing reference.

Purpose

This comparison examines four major hurricanes—Irma (2017), Michael (2018), Florence (2018), and Ian (2022)—using a consistent stage-based lens. The goal is to surface recurring post-award execution patterns and to identify where delivery stability appears to be constrained by upstream readiness conditions.

Shared structural patterns

  • Time lag between landfall and full long-term recovery capitalization.
  • Intake volumes exceeding early execution capacity, creating backlogs before production stabilizes.
  • Delivery variability correlating with upstream readiness (intake discipline, inspections, permitting, payment cadence).
  • Survivor experience dominated by extended displacement rather than a clear staged progression.

Key differences by storm

Storm Dominant execution constraint signals Distinctive system features
Irma (FL, 2017) Statewide scale, intake saturation, evolving program mechanics during rollout Centralized design across diverse local conditions; simultaneous regional surge
Michael (FL, 2018) Rural market capacity, contractor scarcity, long cycle times Catastrophic wind damage in smaller markets; workforce and supply constraints dominate pacing
Florence (NC, 2018) Flood remediation complexity, inspection throughput, gating requirements Stabilization-to-recovery boundary blurs; remediation and environmental review shape delivery tempo
Ian (FL, 2022) Parallel program structures, coordination overhead, post-pandemic labor/supply constraints Regional catastrophe with multiple recovery entities operating in parallel; execution readiness varies by jurisdiction

Execution insight

Across storms, delivery stability appears to track less with generalized contractor quality and more with the timing and integrity of execution readiness: when intake, inspections, permitting flow, and payment mechanics become predictable, throughput and quality stabilize.

Implication for continued study

  • Post-award vendor performance behaves like a downstream signal of system readiness conditions.
  • Recurring execution stress points suggest system behavior that persists across storm types and years.
  • Cross-storm comparability enables clearer identification of where institutional learning did or did not transfer into execution readiness.
