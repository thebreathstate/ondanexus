Case Study

Hurricane Ian (Florida, 2022)

A stage-based observation of post-award execution conditions, delivery variability, and survivor-facing recovery timelines.

By Bob Pudlock, Onda Nexus • FEMA DR-4673-FL • Published: 2026-02-08

Post-award execution Vendor mobilization Delivery variability

This page is derived from the project’s GSPS case study library and is written for public, practitioner-facing reference.

Storm snapshot

Event: Hurricane Ian

Landfall: September 28, 2022

Primary impact areas: Southwest Florida (notably Lee, Charlotte, Collier, Sarasota)

Program context: Large-scale FEMA response followed by long-term recovery programs including HUD CDBG-DR.

What this case study is watching

Ian is useful for systems study because it combined catastrophic damage with multiple recovery entities operating in parallel. The primary interest here is what happens after recovery funding is available and work is awarded: how quickly execution becomes practically available, what constrains early throughput, and what correlates with stable delivery.

Stage observations (high level)

Response and stabilization

Early stages involved large-scale evacuation, sheltering, and FEMA mobilization. Stabilization extended due to severe housing loss, constrained rental availability, and delayed insurance resolution. The result was sustained pressure on recovery systems before long-term programs fully matured.

Program design and capitalization

Long-term recovery design and capitalization introduced a familiar system challenge: time lag between event impact and full program availability. For Ian, parallel program development across state and local entities increased coordination requirements before delivery stabilized.

Vendor mobilization and intake

Vendor mobilization occurred in a high-demand construction environment. Intake pipelines opened under uneven readiness conditions, with early backlogs shaped by eligibility, documentation, environmental review, duplication-of-benefits checks, and permitting dynamics. Execution entry was not synchronized across jurisdictions.

Production and delivery

Delivery behavior varied across jurisdictions and project types. Where upstream conditions stabilized—intake discipline, inspection cadence, permitting flow, and payment mechanics—throughput became more predictable. Where those inputs remained unstable, production variability persisted.

Cross-stage pattern (Ian)

  • Stabilization pressures carried forward into recovery program mobilization.
  • Parallel grantee structures increased coordination overhead before execution stabilized.
  • Delivery variability tracked strongly with upstream readiness conditions, not only with vendor capability.
  • Survivors experienced recovery primarily as extended displacement rather than staged resolution.

Open questions for comparison

  • When was execution practically available across programs, and what were the gating constraints earlier?
  • How did delivery behavior differ once intake, inspections, and permitting stabilized?
  • How do Ian’s post-award dynamics compare to Irma (statewide scale) and Michael (rural market capacity)?
