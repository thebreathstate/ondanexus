Case Study

Hurricane Florence (North Carolina, 2018)

A stage-based observation of flood-driven recovery timelines, remediation complexity, and inspection throughput effects.

By Bob Pudlock, Onda Nexus • FEMA DR-4393-NC • Published: 2026-02-08

Flood-dominated recovery Remediation complexity Inspection throughput

This page is derived from the project’s GSPS case study library and is written for public, practitioner-facing reference.

Storm snapshot

Event: Hurricane Florence

Landfall: September 14, 2018

Primary impact areas: Eastern North Carolina (coastal and inland flooding)

Impact context: Flooding, water intrusion, and long-duration housing uninhabitability shaped recovery timelines.

Program context: FEMA response followed by North Carolina long-term recovery programs including HUD CDBG-DR.

Why Florence is a useful systems case

Florence shows how flood-dominated impacts change recovery behavior. Flooding often blurs the line between stabilization and long-term recovery, and remediation complexity (mold, structural drying, environmental review) can become a throughput driver independent of construction effort.

Stage observations (high level)

Response and stabilization

Florence unfolded over time through prolonged rainfall and inland flooding. Stabilization extended as homes remained uninhabitable due to water intrusion and mold. Temporary housing reliance and limited habitable rental stock shaped survivor timelines.

Program design and capitalization

Long-term recovery design included eligibility and environmental review complexity typical of flood impacts. Time lag between the event and full program availability shaped early execution pacing and widened the gap between demand and delivery.

Vendor mobilization and intake

Intake pipelines opened into large case backlogs. The transition from intake to construction was often slowed by inspection cadence, remediation requirements, and documentation gating that is common in flood-dominated recoveries.

Production and delivery

Production pacing remained slow relative to demand, with long cycle times. Elevated rework emerged due to mold and structural remediation complexity. Delivery stability improved gradually as gating constraints became predictable and inspection rhythms matured.

Cross-stage pattern (Florence)

  • Flood impacts blurred the stabilization-to-recovery boundary, extending interim timelines.
  • Inspection and remediation complexity became throughput drivers.
  • Intake saturation preceded delivery instability, especially where gating requirements were heavy.
  • Vendor performance tracked system gating and inspection capacity more than effort alone.

Open questions for comparison
