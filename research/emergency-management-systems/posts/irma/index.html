Case Study

Hurricane Irma (Florida, 2017)

A stage-based observation of statewide scale, intake saturation signals, and post-award delivery stabilization.

By Bob Pudlock, Onda Nexus • FEMA DR-4337-FL • Published: 2026-02-08

Statewide footprint Intake saturation Centralized program design

This page is derived from the project’s GSPS case study library and is written for public, practitioner-facing reference.

Storm snapshot

Event: Hurricane Irma

Landfall: September 10, 2017

Primary impact areas: Statewide Florida impacts, including South Florida, Central Florida, and the Florida Keys

Program context: Large-scale FEMA response followed by statewide long-term recovery programs including HUD CDBG-DR.

Why Irma is a useful systems case

Irma’s statewide footprint created simultaneous surge across multiple regions, compressing early response and stabilization stages and shaping long-term recovery design. This makes Irma a strong case for observing how scale affects mobilization, intake, and delivery stability after award.

Stage observations (high level)

Response and stabilization

Early phases involved mass evacuation, sheltering, widespread power disruption, and fuel/logistics constraints. Stabilization extended as housing demand increased across the state, with insurance and assistance timelines overlapping and rental markets tightening in many areas.

Program design and capitalization

Long-term recovery design was centralized at the state level, improving consistency and control. As programs scaled, administrative complexity and evolving rules shaped how quickly delivery could stabilize across diverse local conditions.

Vendor mobilization and intake

Intake pipelines opened under heavy volume. Early signals included eligibility and documentation backlogs and ramp-up challenges before execution stabilized. In practical terms, Irma illustrates how post-award performance is often shaped by intake saturation and the timing of execution readiness.

Production and delivery

Delivery behavior varied across regions and project types. Over time, throughput improved as processes stabilized. Early variability aligned with front-loaded demand and program evolution during implementation.

Cross-stage pattern (Irma)

  • Statewide scale compressed early stages and increased coordination and logistics pressures.
  • Centralized design improved consistency but reduced flexibility in local execution pacing.
  • Intake volumes outpaced early execution capacity, which preceded delivery variability.
  • Stabilization improved as intake, inspection, and administrative rhythms became predictable.

Open questions for comparison

  • How did centralized program design affect mobilization speed and delivery stability?
  • At what point did intake pressure decline relative to production capacity?
  • Which dynamics reappear in Ian (multi-grantee) and Michael (rural market capacity)?
