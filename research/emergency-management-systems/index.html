Research Project

Emergency Management Systems Performance (FEMA + CDBG-DR)

A stage-based study of post-award execution across major Southeast storms—focused on delivery reliability, survivor outcomes, and stewardship of public funds.

By Bob Pudlock, Onda Nexus • Updated: 2026-02-08

Execution-side perspective Survivor-centered outcomes Cross-storm comparability

This project compiles and analyzes publicly observable performance patterns in large-scale disaster response and long-term recovery, with a specific interest in what happens after work is awarded: vendor mobilization, throughput, quality, and delivery timelines.

The method uses a consistent stage-based structure to compare storms without changing the lens, so recurring execution stress points become visible across different geographies, years, and program architectures.

Project introduction

Disaster survivors experience recovery as time: waiting, displacement, uncertainty, and the slow return of normal life. Agencies and vendors experience recovery as stages: response, stabilization, program design, mobilization, delivery, and oversight. This research connects those views by observing how execution performance behaves across the full lifecycle—especially at the point where funding exists and work has been awarded, but delivery remains fragile or uneven.

Questions guiding the work

  • At what point is execution practically available after award—and what prevents it earlier?
  • When performance degrades post-award, which constraints are upstream system constraints versus downstream execution constraints?
  • How does intake saturation show up before production instability?
  • What conditions correlate with stable throughput and quality once delivery begins?
  • Across storms, where do similar execution stress points recur—and what evidence suggests lessons transferred (or did not)?

These questions are approached through observation and cross-storm comparison using publicly available materials, program updates, and oversight reporting.
